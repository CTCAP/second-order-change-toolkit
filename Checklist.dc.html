Second-Order Change Checklist

A diagnostic tool for reading emails and conversations, after Bateson and Watzlawick.

First-order Second-order
What changes Amount, frequency, wording, process step, who-does-what task The rule set itself: authority, role definition, what the relationship is
Feels like Tinkering, adjusting, doing it better/more/faster A discontinuity, a jump, a different game
Typical language “Let's clarify,” “next time please,” “I'll take more initiative” “This isn't a communication gap, it's a structural gap”
Risk “More of the same” — intensifying a fix that hasn't worked Reframe is asserted but doesn't open new options (disguised first-order)

The checklist

1  Locate the punctuation
  • Whose account of “what started this” is embedded in the message?
  • Is it stated as settled fact, or is it visibly one reading among possible others?
  • Could the other party punctuate the same sequence of events differently, and still be right from where they sit?
2  Classify the move
  • Does the message adjust something within the existing rules (amount, process, wording)? → first-order
  • Does the message propose changing the rules themselves (who holds authority, what the role means, what the relationship is)? → second-order
  • Does it use second-order language (structure, trust, role) but the actual ask is still “do the existing thing better/more/faster”? → disguised first-order — flag this, especially in your own drafts
3  Check for “more of the same” traps
  • Is the proposed fix structurally identical to something already tried, just intensified? (more meetings, more clarifying emails, more documentation of scope)
  • Is the attempted solution plausibly part of the problem rather than the cure for it?
4  Check for double-bind structure
  • Are there two injunctions operating at different levels that can't both be honoured? (e.g. “you're accountable” at the role level + “run it by me first” at the interaction level)
  • Is naming the contradiction itself treated as the violation, rather than as legitimate feedback?
  • Is there a way to respond that isn't penalized either way?
5  Test the reframe — don't just assert it
  • If the other party fully accepted this frame, what new moves become possible that weren't possible before?
  • If the honest answer is “none, it's the same ask restated” — it's first-order wearing second-order clothes.
6  Decide whether to name the level
  • Would naming the level explicitly help? (“This isn't about the travel budget, it's about how budget authority gets decided.”)
  • Or would naming it trigger defensiveness, such that an oblique reframe — changing the frame without announcing you're doing it — lands better?
  • Either way: is this a deliberate choice, or a default you fell into?
7  Sustainability check
  • If this reframe is accepted, what stops the system reverting to first-order the next time a similar issue arises?
  • Does the message build in any way of re-challenging the new frame later, or does it treat the reframe as a one-off fix?
Fast triage version

For a quick read of a single email or exchange, three questions:

  1. Level check — is this asking for more of the same, or a different rule?
  2. Trap check — has this exact fix been tried before, just less intensely?
  3. Reframe check — does accepting this frame open new options, or just relabel the old ask?

Framework built from Bateson's logical types, Watzlawick/Weakland/Fisch's Change: Principles of Problem Formation and Problem Resolution (1974), and Bartunek & Moch's schema-based extension into organizational development (1987).
