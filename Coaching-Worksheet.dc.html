Reading the Pattern Underneath the Problem

A tool for spotting whether what you're dealing with needs a better fix within the current rules — or a change to the rules themselves.

The idea in brief

Some problems respond to doing the same thing better, faster, or more often. Others don't — no matter how well you execute the existing approach, the problem persists or gets worse, because the approach itself is part of the pattern keeping the problem alive.

Psychologists Paul Watzlawick and colleagues, building on Gregory Bateson's work, called these two kinds of change first-order and second-order. Telling them apart — in a conflict, a stuck project, or a recurring frustration — is often the whole ballgame.

First-order change
What changes — Amount, frequency, wording, process, who-does-what
Feels like — Tinkering, adjusting, doing it better or more
Risk — “More of the same”: pushing harder on a fix that isn't working
Second-order change
What changes — The rules themselves: authority, role, what the relationship is
Feels like — A discontinuity: a different game, not a better version of the old one
Risk — A reframe that sounds different but doesn't open new options
Walk the situation through

Seven Questions

Bring a specific situation to mind — an email, a recurring argument, a stuck project. Work through each step. Don't rush the write-in lines; that's where the real thinking happens.

1  Locate the punctuation
Everyone in a conflict has a story about what started it — and both stories can be locally true.
☐  What's your account of what started this?
☐  What might the other person's account be?
☐  Where do the two accounts diverge?
2  Classify the move
Is the fix on the table an adjustment, or a change to the rules?
☐  Does the proposed fix stay within the existing way of doing things?
☐  Or does it change who decides, what the role means, or what the relationship is?
☐  Is it dressed up in “bigger” language but actually the same old ask?
3  Check for “more of the same”
The attempted solution is sometimes the problem, intensified.
☐  Has something like this fix been tried before, just less intensely?
☐  Is the current approach possibly part of what's keeping the problem alive?
4  Check for a double bind
Two instructions that can't both be honoured — and naming the contradiction gets treated as the offence.
☐  Are there two expectations here that can't both be met?
☐  Does raising that tension get read as the problem, rather than as useful information?
5  Test the reframe
A real reframe opens up new moves. A fake one just relabels the same complaint.
☐  If the new frame were accepted, what could you do that you couldn't do before?
☐  If the answer is “nothing new” — it's not actually a second-order move yet.
6  Decide whether to name it
Sometimes naming the shift helps. Sometimes it's better made without announcing it.
☐  Would naming this out loud help, or trigger defensiveness?
☐  What's the deliberate choice here — not the default reaction?
7  Sustainability check
A new frame can quietly slide back into an old pattern if nothing protects it.
☐  What stops this reverting to the old pattern next time?
☐  How will the new frame get revisited, rather than treated as a one-off fix?
