When Solutions in Ministry Address the Wrong Problem

A reflection for ministry leaders on solutions, grace, and change.

In the early nineties, I oversaw the start of a new congregation on a Sunday evening. We wanted it to reach younger people, and we were small in number, so we met in the church hall, painted, and rearranged the furniture to signal the shift. It felt like a small, sensible move at the time.

Years later, long after I'd left, I ran into the minister who took it over. He was frustrated. The room was exactly as we'd left it, and nobody would touch it. Whenever anyone raised the idea of rearranging it again, someone would say I'd told them this layout was what reached young people, so it shouldn't change. A solution meant to free things up had hardened into the thing nobody was allowed to touch. They were stuck. Situations like this one have led me to ask why some ministry solutions end up making change more challenging.

Why do some ministry problems get worse the harder you try to solve them?

Church life throws up problems of every kind, and over the years I've listened to hundreds of leaders talk through the solutions they've tried when they have been stuck. Sometimes I've been one of them, going back again and again to a problem that stays stubbornly in place no matter how much effort goes into it.

One way through that stuckness is to ask what kind of change we're actually trying to make. There are two kinds, and when a situation gets stuck, it's usually because we've applied the wrong one.

Two kinds of change

Paul Watzlawick, of the Mental Research Institute in Palo Alto, called them first-order and second-order change.1

His illustration of first-order change is insomnia. Try to force yourself to sleep, and you find yourself lying awake, counting sheep. Sleep comes on its own terms. But when it doesn't, the insomniac reaches for more and more solutions: less screen time, more meditation, medication. Any of these may be sensible in themselves, but each is another attempt to solve the problem within the same frame. Sometimes the solution itself becomes part of the problem. An overdependence on medication, say.

That's first-order change. It alters what happens within a system without changing the system itself. You do more or less of something. You work harder, become more efficient, communicate more clearly, and tighten the process. You accept the system as it is, and it remains unchanged.

Most of what we call problem-solving works this way, and usually it should. The governance already exists, it simply needs to be applied. It's like calling a plumber when a pipe leaks, or improving an order of service that isn't flowing well. Skill and experience generally make things better because the problem sits within a stable system.

Second-order change is different. It changes the system itself.

Watzlawick tells the story of a nineteenth-century Parisian army commander ordered to clear a city square by firing on “the rabble.”1 He could have repeated the order or escalated the threat. Instead, he announced that he could see only respectable citizens before him and asked whether they would mind stepping aside so he could get a clear shot at the rabble.

Within minutes, the square was empty.

He never argued with the crowd or changed the command. He changed the category in which they understood themselves. Once that happened, the old frame could no longer continue.

There's a therapy-room version of the same move, from an early-1960s case treated by Don Jackson, co-founder of the Mental Research Institute.2 A man in his forties, in a responsible position, had gone on sick leave, convinced that government agencies were watching him. His phone was tapped, his mail was followed, and his house was under surveillance. Early in the intake session, he remarked, almost as an aside, that the consulting room itself was probably bugged.

The obvious response would have been to challenge the belief or ask for evidence. Jackson did the opposite. He got up and announced he wasn't willing to let anyone violate the privacy of his work with patients. They weren't going anywhere until they'd found the bug. He started searching the room. The patient watched, amused, until Jackson's insistence eventually drew him in to help with the search himself. Not long after, the man asked him to stop, sat down, and started talking about his marital problems instead. Jackson never argued him out of his belief. He entered the frame so completely that it collapsed under its own weight.

The pattern I watch for in myself

Stuck patterns like this turn up in ministries all the time. A second-order problem gets treated as though it were a first-order problem. Leaders respond with greater diligence, more biblical teaching on a particular theme, another process, clearer communication, more meetings, and better planning. Yet if the problem lies in the structure of the situation itself, those responses often strengthen the very pattern they're trying to change. Watzlawick called this “more of the same.” In my experience, it's one of the most common ways organisations unintentionally keep themselves stuck.1

I see it most clearly in how we form leaders.3 We see a gap and add a module with more insights, more pre-reading, and more time spent together in a classroom where genuine bonds are formed. It is wonderful. The content and trainers get great feedback, as they should. It's excellent. But six months later, the person who has been trained leads under pressure exactly as they did before. Our instinct is to assume we haven't taught enough yet, so we teach more or change the technique. But the real issue usually isn't information. Formation takes sustained relationship, developmental heat, and dependence on the Spirit. More content can be a first-order fix for a second-order problem. We can work on the curriculum forever and never touch what actually needs to change.

Churches and ministries run on first-order change most of the time, because that's what most situations require. However, I am becoming increasingly aware of how easy it is to mistake one kind of problem for another.

So now, more often than before, I find myself asking different questions. If the conversation has genuinely reframed the situation, what has become possible that wasn't possible before? If the answer is not much, the frame hasn't changed. We've simply described the same problem differently. That may be all that is needed, but at times it is not enough.

There's another danger as well. Even genuine second-order change doesn't sustain itself. Organisations naturally absorb new ways of thinking back into old patterns. New language slowly acquires old meanings. Structures adapt until the original insight becomes another routine. Second-order change has to be revisited. It cannot simply be announced.

The gospel as second-order change

There's a parallel in the gospel.

Tim Keller helped us see that moralism and relativism appear to be opposites but are built on the same foundation. One seeks salvation by keeping the rules. The other is by writing new ones. Both remain strategies of self-salvation.4 Moralism and relativism are not really alternatives to each other, but two versions of the same underlying move, and the gospel doesn't sit anywhere on the spectrum between them. It refuses the terms of the argument altogether.5 The gospel does not offer a better version of either strategy. It changes the frame.

You no longer stand before God on the basis of your performance but on Christ's. Paul makes this clear with his own résumé in Philippians 3. The very things he once counted as righteousness, he now counts as loss “because of the surpassing worth of knowing Christ Jesus” (Phil. 3:7–9). That's not self-improvement. The whole question of self-salvation is displaced rather than improved. The old frame no longer applies. Paul says, “If anyone is in Christ, he is a new creation. The old has passed away; behold, the new has come” (2 Cor. 5:17). Not a renovated system, but a different one.

Jonathan Edwards helps ground this further. In his 1734 sermon A Divine and Supernatural Light, he described the difference between knowing that honey is sweet and actually tasting it.6 You can understand the gospel completely and still live inside the old frame, still trying to secure yourself through competence, success, approval, or control. You know the truth, but it hasn't yet changed what drives you.

This is why Paul prays not just for right belief but for minds actually renewed. “Do not be conformed to this world, but be transformed by the renewal of your mind” (Rom. 12:2). And it's why the fear that drives so much first-order effort — the fear of not being enough, not being safe, not being secure — only loosens its grip when displaced by something else entirely. “There is no fear in love, but perfect love casts out fear… whoever fears has not been perfected in love” (1 John 4:18).

That's why the gospel isn't simply another leadership principle to add to the list. It's the only thing that finally frees us from needing our leadership to save us.

Application

The next time you find yourself stuck, resist the instinct to reach immediately for another solution. Ask instead whether you're treating a second-order problem as though it were a first-order one. Is this really a matter of more effort, clearer communication, or a tighter process? Or is the frame itself the problem?

Most of us reach for first-order solutions at the wrong time because something in us needs them to work. So does being right, succeeding, staying in control, or being approved of. That is why second-order change begins with our own hearts before it begins with our organisations. Only people whose security rests in Christ are genuinely free to stop defending the existing frame. Once our standing no longer depends on making the system work, we can ask different questions, take different risks, and invite others into a different way of seeing.

That's where the possibilities for second-order change come from. Not from technique, but from security. Once Christ is no longer simply an idea we affirm but the one in whom our confidence rests, the old patterns begin to lose their attraction. We're no longer compelled to keep operating inside the old systems, and we can invite others into a genuinely new way of being and doing.
