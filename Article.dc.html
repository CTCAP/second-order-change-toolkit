The Fix That Becomes the Problem

On why some organisational problems get worse the harder you try to solve them

Church life throws up many kinds of issues, from deeply pastoral and personal ones right through to graveyards and property disputes. I've also listened to hundreds of leaders try every kind of solution to the problems in front of them. Sometimes I've been one of them, trying again and again to deal with something that just won't be dealt with.

One way through this stuckness is to see it from a particular angle. There are two different kinds of change, and most of the effort we pour into a stuck situation is the wrong kind for the problem we've got.

Two kinds of change

Paul Watzlawick, working out of the Mental Research Institute, gave them a name: first-order change and second-order change.

His illustration of first-order change is insomnia. Try to force sleep by sheer will and you get the opposite: wide awake, watching the clock. Sleep only ever comes on its own — it can't be commanded into being. But the insomniac, growing more desperate as the night wears on, keeps trying anyway. He adjusts his diet, changes his bedtime routine and eventually reaches for medication. Each of those moves, however sensible on its own, is still just trying harder at the same game. Every step meant to solve the problem deepens it instead.

That's first-order change: working inside a system without touching the system's own rules. You do more of something, less of something, or do it faster or more clearly. The game stays the same; only the score moves. Most of what leaders call problem-solving is first-order, and most of the time that's exactly right. A slow process gets streamlined. A vague brief gets clarified. There is nothing wrong with the rules; the execution needed work. It's like calling a plumber to fix a pipe or tightening up an order of service — there may be more skilled or sensitive ways to do it, but the parameters are predictable, and so are the solutions.

Second-order change is a different kind of move — it changes the system itself rather than optimising a move within it. Watzlawick tells a story from nineteenth-century Paris that shows what this looks like. An army commander is ordered to clear a city square by firing on “the rabble.” Instead of shouting the order louder or escalating the threat — adding more force to more of the same — he does something else entirely. He tells the crowd he has orders to fire on the rabble, but since he sees only honest, respectable citizens in front of him, would they mind stepping aside so he can get a clear shot at the rabble? The square empties in minutes. Nobody in it was rabble a moment before. He didn't ask anyone to do anything different. He changed which category they were standing in, and the old game became impossible to keep playing.

There is a therapy-room version of the same move that I once read. Don Jackson, one of the founding figures at the Mental Research Institute, saw a man referred by his employer for insisting he was under government surveillance. He believed his phone was bugged and his house was being watched. Early in the session, the man mentioned almost in passing that he assumed the room itself was bugged, then suggested they carry on regardless. The expected first-order response would be to challenge the belief by asking what evidence he had and gently probing the delusion. Jackson did the opposite. He sat up, visibly irritated, and refused to continue until they'd found the bug. Then he got down to check the drawers and the underside of the desk, ordering the startled patient to help him look. After several minutes, the man gave up the search himself, sat back down and said what he'd actually come to talk about: his marriage was falling apart. Jackson never argued the man out of his belief. He simply took it more seriously than the man himself was willing to, and the frame organising the whole session quietly stopped working. The patient was no longer guarded while the therapist investigated him. That's a second-order move: changing the game being played, rather than subjecting the delusion to more scrutiny.

Here is the pattern I've watched. A second-order problem shows up wearing first-order clothes, and leaders answer it with more diligence, more explanation, more process and more care. Because the problem is structural, rather than a matter of execution, the extra effort doesn't touch it. Often it feeds it. Watzlawick has a name for this: “more of the same.” In my experience, it's the single most common way good people entrench a problem they're genuinely trying to solve.

The pattern I watch for in myself

Organisations run on first-order change most of the time, and that's healthy. You don't want every friction point escalated into an identity crisis for the whole system. But there is a specific trap I watch for in my own leadership as much as in the leaders I coach: reaching for second-order language to make a first-order ask. We talk about trust, structure, role clarity or culture, while the actual request underneath is still just to do the existing thing, but better. It sounds like a deeper conversation. It functions as more of the same wearing a nicer outfit.

The test I use on my own drafts before I send them is blunt: if the other person fully accepted my reframe, what could either of us do that we couldn't do before? If the honest answer is nothing — if it's the same request restated in richer vocabulary — I haven't found a second-order move. I've dressed up a first-order one.

There is a second trap on the far side of that one. Once a real reframe has landed — bringing a genuine shift in how authority, role or relationship is understood — it doesn't stay landed by itself. Leave it alone and the system will slowly digest even a good new frame back into old habits. Sustaining second-order change means returning to the new frame on purpose, again and again, rather than declaring it once and walking away.

A question worth sitting with

Next time you're stuck in a pattern that effort alone won't shift — whether it's a recurring dispute, a role that keeps drifting back to where it started, or a conversation that circles without ever landing — pause before reaching for the next iteration of the same fix. Don't ask, “How do I do this harder?” Ask whether you've been treating a second-order problem as if enough first-order effort would eventually close the gap.

Sometimes the honest answer really is that you need to try harder. But sometimes trying harder is the loop itself, and the way out isn't more of the same. It's a different question altogether.
